Section 01 · The Rhythm
When does crime happen?

A city of two shifts

[PLACEHOLDER — Introduce the central finding: San Francisco's crime landscape splits neatly along the clock. Petty theft (Larceny-theft) dominates daytime hours — peaking between 11am and 3pm, when foot traffic is highest in commercial districts. But as the sun sets, the character of crime shifts. Assault and Vandalism surge after 9pm, reaching their peak between midnight and 2am. This "two-shift" pattern holds consistently across all 23 years of data.]

[PLACEHOLDER — 1–2 sentences connecting to the reader's lived experience: tourists having pockets picked on Market Street at noon vs. the different dangers of walking through the Tenderloin after midnight.]

Larceny-theft — the city's most common crime — is overwhelmingly a daytime phenomenon. Assault is overwhelmingly a crime of the night.

Section 02 · The Map
Where does crime concentrate?

Not all neighborhoods are equal

[PLACEHOLDER — Introduce geographic inequality. The Tenderloin, Mission, and Southern districts consistently appear at the top of every crime ranking. But the story isn't simply "bad neighborhoods." The Southern district's high numbers partly reflect the density of SoMa — tech offices, bars, and transit hubs. The Tenderloin reflects decades of under-investment and concentrated poverty. Frame for the reader: the map is not just a crime map, it's a map of the city's unresolved tensions.]

Section 03 · The Long Arc
How has crime evolved over 23 years?

Shocks, shifts, and a city in flux

[PLACEHOLDER — Introduce the long-term narrative. Three major shocks reshape the trend lines: (1) the 2008 financial crisis, which correlates with a modest uptick in property crime; (2) the post-2015 tech-boom gentrification, which reshapes which neighborhoods see crime; and (3) the COVID-19 pandemic (2020), which causes the single most dramatic single-year drop in reported crime across almost every category. Conclusion
What it all means

The city never sleeps — but it doesn't always feel the same

[PLACEHOLDER — Synthesis paragraph. Tie together the three findings: (1) crime in SF is deeply rhythmic — governed by the clock and the calendar; (2) geography matters enormously — a handful of districts absorb a disproportionate share of all reported incidents; (3) the long arc of crime is shaped by external shocks (pandemic, financial crises) and by deliberate policy choices (drug legalization). The data paints a portrait of a city in constant tension between its prosperity and its persistent inequality.]
